1. About the Project

This project explores human activity patterns in London throughout 2020, a year marked by the unprecedented global COVID-19 pandemic. By analyzing daily movement data and focusing on Polygons of Interest (POIs), such as transit hubs, parks, and event venues, we aim to understand how the pandemic influenced urban mobility. Our analysis offers insights into the reduction of activity during lockdowns, the rebound effects post-restrictions, and the shifting dynamics of human movement in response to public health measures.

2. Data Acquistion and Preprocessing

The foundation of this project is the 2020 movement data of London, which provides daily aggregated metrics of human activity. These data points are complemented by geospatial information, including: Geo-coordinates linked to specific locations, London borough boundaries, and Points of Interest (POIs) categorized by function and significance. Preprocessing involved cleaning, mapping, and aggregating the data for analysis. Special focus was given to temporal patterns, allowing us to identify activity trends before, during, and after key COVID-19 milestones, such as lockdown announcements or easing measures.

2.3 Temporal Data

The Temporal Data section highlights how mobility patterns in London evolved throughout the different phases of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020. By dividing the year into distinct intervals aligned with major policy changes and restrictions, we analyze the short- and long-term effects of lockdowns, reopenings, and other interventions on human activity.

2.3.1 Corona Intervals

To better understand mobility changes, the year 2020 has been divided into seven key intervals, each corresponding to major COVID-19 measures or milestones. The time intervals are based on governmental documents that can be found here.

  • Pre-Corona (Jan 1 - Mar 25): Baseline period before any pandemic-related restrictions.
  • First Lockdown (Mar 26 - May 10): Strict nationwide lockdown causing sharp declines in movement.
  • Return to Relaxing Restrictions (May 11 - Oct 13): Gradual easing of restrictions leading to activity rebounds.
  • Three-Tier System (Oct 14 - Nov 4): Regionalized restrictions based on infection rates.
  • Second Lockdown (Nov 5 - Dec 1): Reimposition of nationwide restrictions.
  • End of Second Lockdown (Dec 2 - Dec 20): Temporary recovery under tiered restrictions.
  • Four-Tier System in London (Dec 21 - Dec 31): Strict "Stay at Home" orders applied to London.

4. Discussion

4.1 Spatio-Temporal Patterns

All analytical approaches corroborate that mobility patterns in London underwent significant changes with the onset of the pandemic. On one hand, there was a sharp decline in mobility, particularly in the city center and around major Points of Interest (POIs). On the other hand, the remaining mobility was slightly redistributed into residential areas and parks. The negative Moran’s I values along major traffic arteries reinforce this observation: during restrictive phases, streets were utilized less compared to their surroundings, while activity levels in adjacent areas, such as residential neighborhoods, remained relatively stable.


4.2 Impact on POIs

Whether it’s subway stations, bus depots, parks, or tourist attractions, highly frequented POIs experienced substantial declines during the initial lockdown phase. Although a partial recovery was noted during the summer months when restrictions were relaxed, activity levels generally remained below pre-pandemic norms. Repeated lockdowns in the autumn and winter prevented a complete return to previous activity levels. Public transportation hubs, such as Waterloo Station, consistently demonstrated sensitivity to restrictions, as evidenced by the development of clusters around these central stations. This indicates that public transport usage was significantly affected by lockdown measures, impacting overall mobility patterns in central London.


4.3 Limitations

Several limitations impacted the scope and accuracy of this study. The use of WGS84 coordinates introduced distortions in distance calculations, particularly affecting vertical clustering patterns. Converting the movement data to a metric coordinate system is recommended for future research to enhance clustering precision. Additionally, computational constraints limited the analysis to smaller geographic areas and shorter time intervals, preventing a more comprehensive large-scale analysis. Enhanced computational resources and refined parameter testing could address these issues, allowing for more robust and detailed insights into mobility patterns.

5. Conclusion

5.1 Summary of Findings

The combined methodologies—time interval analysis, global and local Moran’s I, POI evaluation, and ST-DBSCAN clustering—provide a multifaceted view of mobility in London during the pandemic. Key insights include:

  • Lockdown Phases: Led to a significant reduction in spatial cluster density and a more uniform distribution of mobility.
  • Relaxation Phases: Resulted in a partial return of known hotspots, though often not to pre-pandemic intensity levels.
  • Transportation and Tourism Centers: Particularly sensitive to restrictions, while residential neighborhoods and parks temporarily gained prominence in mobility patterns.
  • Methodological Factors: The choice of coordinate system and parameter settings, along with technical resources, critically influenced the clustering outcomes.

These findings highlight the profound impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on urban mobility patterns and underscore the importance of employing targeted spatio-temporal analytical methods with appropriate data and computational support. Future analyses aimed at urban planning or tourism management should focus on refining the data foundation and conducting more comprehensive parameter testing to minimize inaccuracies and fully leverage the potential of spatio-temporal clustering techniques.


5.2 Future Work

While our analysis provided valuable insights into mobility patterns in London during the COVID-19 pandemic, several areas for improvement remain. Future research could focus on the following aspects:

Refinement of Spatial Analysis: Converting the dataset from WGS84 to a metric coordinate system (e.g., UTM) would improve clustering precision and ensure consistent distance-based analysis. This step is crucial for more accurate identification of movement hotspots.

Optimization of Clustering Parameters: Our ST-DBSCAN analysis was limited by predefined parameters. Future studies could implement automated tuning techniques, such as grid search or machine learning-based optimization, to improve the detection of meaningful mobility clusters.

Higher Temporal Resolution: Currently, movement patterns are analyzed in fixed time intervals. Introducing rolling windows or finer time granularity (e.g., hourly data) could provide deeper insights into mobility shifts during key transition phases, such as lockdown announcements.

Integration with External Data Sources: Incorporating additional datasets, such as public transport usage, economic activity, or weather conditions, could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the factors influencing movement trends.

Interactive and Dynamic Visualizations: Further improvements in data visualization—such as animated time-series movement maps or user-controlled filtering—could enhance accessibility and interpretability for policymakers and researchers.

Expansion to Other Geographic Areas: Applying this methodology to other cities or regions would enable a comparative study of how different urban environments responded to mobility restrictions, providing broader insights into the impact of large-scale disruptions.

By implementing these improvements, future research can further enhance our understanding of urban mobility dynamics and contribute to more effective urban planning and crisis management strategies.
